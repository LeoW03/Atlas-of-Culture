Why Aid Can Entrench Power

Three mechanisms — and one catastrophic proof of concept

Mechanism 01
The Accountability Bypass
Governments that collect taxes from citizens answer to them. Governments funded by foreign aid answer to donors instead. Aid replaces the accountability loop. Citizens lose leverage.
Mechanism 02
Dutch Disease for Governance
Large inflows crowd out domestic revenue. Why build a tax authority when donors keep the lights on? Institutional capacity atrophies from disuse. The state becomes a conduit, not a government.
Mechanism 03
Strategic Aid ≠ Humanitarian Aid
The largest recipients are often the most geopolitically important, not the most suffering. Egypt: $1.3bn/yr from the US, mostly military. Governance has declined steadily.
Case Study · Afghanistan
$82 billion over 20 years built a government that existed primarily on paper. Salaries flowed to "ghost soldiers" who didn't exist. Contracts went to warlords who subcontracted to the Taliban. Aid funded the patronage networks it was supposed to dismantle. When the money stopped, so did the state. Two weeks after US withdrawal in August 2021, the government dissolved.

Counterexample · Rwanda
Aid Can Work — With Conditions
Rwanda is a major recipient with genuinely improving governance. Difference: strong domestic institutions, enforced accountability, aid tied to measurable outcomes. Structure matters as much as money.
Counterexample · Botswana
Low Aid, High Governance
Built institutions on diamond revenue — accountable to citizens because it needed their legitimacy. WB corruption score: +0.7. Among Africa's highest. Aid dependence: minimal. Fiscal contract intact.

Data Sources

  • OECD DAC Creditor Reporting System — Official Development Assistance flows by donor, recipient, and sector; data through 2023.
  • World Bank Worldwide Governance Indicators — Six aggregate governance dimensions—including Control of Corruption and Government Effectiveness—for 200+ countries; data through 2023.
  • Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index — Annual composite index of perceived public-sector corruption across 180 countries, scored 0–100; 2024 edition.
  • AidData — Tracking Underreported Financial Flows — Research lab dataset linking bilateral and multilateral aid commitments to governance and development outcomes, with project-level data through 2021.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Reverse causality is a core problem: donors often direct more aid toward fragile, high-corruption states precisely because need is greatest, making a simple correlation between aid levels and poor governance misleading about cause and effect.
  • The Corruption Perceptions Index measures expert and business perceptions of corruption, not directly observed corrupt acts — it can reflect media coverage and geopolitical framing as much as actual governance quality.
  • Aid is not monolithic: budget support disbursed directly to governments has very different governance implications than project aid delivered through NGOs or tied humanitarian assistance, but aggregate aid figures obscure these distinctions.
