04 — Analysis

Why Turnout Doesn't Explain Satisfaction

01 / 04

The Measurement Paradox

Voter turnout measures input into a system. Democratic satisfaction measures how citizens feel about the system's output. These are fundamentally different dimensions — one behavioral, one evaluative.

Conflating them assumes that voting is the primary channel through which people form opinions about democracy. The data suggests otherwise: what matters more is whether institutions feel responsive, whether courts are trusted, whether local governance actually works.

02 / 04

Why Switzerland Is the Key Outlier

Switzerland's 45% parliamentary turnout masks a fundamentally different democratic architecture. Swiss citizens vote in 4–6 national referenda per year, plus cantonal and municipal votes. Turnout per issue is low; the number of issues citizens decide is extraordinarily high.

Swiss direct democracy creates persistent agency — the feeling that you could vote on almost anything that affects you, at almost any time. That sense of available power, more than the act of voting, drives 83% satisfaction.

03 / 04

Compulsory Voting and Its Limits

Australia, Belgium, Brazil, and Uruguay all have compulsory voting — and all show moderate-to-low satisfaction despite high turnout (55%, 50%, 41%, 55% respectively). You can legally compel participation. You cannot compel the feeling of being heard.

Compulsory voting inflates turnout without necessarily creating the institutional feedback loops that make citizens feel their participation changes anything. The ballot box is not the bottleneck.

04 / 04

What Actually Predicts Democratic Satisfaction

Cross-national research (Dalton, Norris, Inglehart) points to: institutional trust (courts, parliament, press), perceived responsiveness (do politicians listen?), corruption levels, and economic security.

The US scores 55% turnout but just 30% satisfaction — not because Americans vote too little, but because a significant majority believe the system serves interests other than theirs. Turnout can't fix that. Institutional reform can.
