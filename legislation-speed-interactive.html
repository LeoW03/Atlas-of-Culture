Why these numbers are not directly comparable
US vs everyone
Congress delegates more than it legislates
The US counts enacted public laws but excludes executive orders, agency rules, and the massive volume of regulation shifted to agencies since the 1970s Legislative Reorganization Act. Congress doing less partly reflects deliberate delegation, not pure dysfunction.
UK Statutory Instruments
Acts are the tip of the iceberg
The UK passes around 25 Acts per year but issues 1,000 to 2,000 Statutory Instruments annually — secondary legislation with full legal force. Comparing Acts alone dramatically understates UK legislative activity.
German federalism
16 state parliaments are invisible here
Germany counts federal laws only; the 16 Länder each have their own parliaments. German federalism distributes lawmaking across more bodies, making federal counts artificially low.
Bicameral friction
Upper houses create structural drag
Australia, Canada, and the UK have upper houses creating friction. Germany’s Bundesrat can block laws affecting state interests. These structural differences explain variation more than political will.
What the trend tells us
The US decline is real — just not the whole story
The US decline since the 1970s does track with rising polarization (DW-NOMINATE scores), increasing filibuster use, and more divided government. The mechanism is real even if raw counts overstate it.

Caveats
  • U.S. data counts laws enacted per two-year Congress, then annualized. Many early "laws" were minor (private bills, commemorative acts). The decline partly reflects Congress shifting private bills and routine matters to executive agencies after the 1970 Legislative Reorganization Act.
  • Passage rate (passed/introduced) is only available for the US, UK, and Canada where introduced-bill counts are reliably tracked. For other countries, the metric shows laws per year regardless of toggle.
  • "Laws passed" counts vary by what each parliament considers a law vs. a regulation, decree, or statutory instrument. The UK's ~25 Acts/year exclude hundreds of Statutory Instruments; Germany's count includes federal laws but not Länder legislation. Direct cross-country comparison of absolute numbers is misleading — the trend within each country is the meaningful signal.
  • Australia and France data before 1970 is sparse in publicly available English-language sources. Earlier years are excluded rather than estimated.
  • The 118th U.S. Congress (2023–24) figure is based on partial data as of late 2024.
