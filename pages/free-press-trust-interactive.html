Two different things

Press freedom measures the legal and structural environment for journalism — whether reporters can work without government interference, imprisonment, or violence. Media trust measures something else entirely: whether ordinary people believe what they read.

These two things should correlate. A free press, in theory, produces more reliable journalism. And to a degree, they do — the countries at the bottom of press freedom rankings (North Korea, Eritrea, Russia) also have very low trust. But among wealthy democracies, the relationship breaks down dramatically.

The US has more press freedom than 130 countries. It has less public trust in media than most of them.

The American anomaly

The US ranks 55th globally on press freedom — below Lithuania, Timor-Leste, and Namibia — but that score (66/100) still places it well above the global median. What's anomalous isn't the freedom score. It's the trust gap.

Only 32% of Americans say they trust the media, according to the Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2024. That's lower than Kenya (61%), Brazil (54%), and Germany (50%). Countries with significantly less press freedom report significantly more trust in their media.

The reasons are structural: extreme media polarization, collapse of local news, social media amplifying distrust, and decades of political attacks on journalism as an institution. A free press that no one believes is a different kind of problem than a censored press.

The Nordic contrast

Finland, Norway, and Denmark consistently rank at the top of both metrics — high freedom and high trust. The common factor isn't just lack of government interference. It's public broadcasting funded at arm's length from government, strong local news ecosystems, and a cultural norm of treating journalism as a public good rather than a partisan product.

The UK is an interesting middle case: ranked 23rd on press freedom, 43% trust — higher than the US despite tabloid culture, because the BBC anchor holds.

What trust actually predicts

Low media trust doesn't mean people consume less media — they consume more, but from more partisan sources. The Reuters Institute finds that low-trust audiences are more likely to actively avoid news (41% of Americans report news avoidance, the highest in the study), even as they spend more time on social media consuming news-adjacent content.

The paradox: the country with one of the world's freest presses has produced a population that's simultaneously drowning in information and retreating from news entirely.

  • These two datasets measure structurally different things. RSF scores the legal and physical environment for journalists — government interference, violence, legal harassment. Reuters measures what ordinary people say when asked if they trust the news. The scatter plot shows correlation, not causation; a country can have a free press that its citizens distrust for reasons entirely unrelated to press freedom.
  • China's reported trust figure (~80%) should be interpreted cautiously. Surveying in an authoritarian context may not capture genuine sentiment, and critical media consumption carries social and legal risk.
