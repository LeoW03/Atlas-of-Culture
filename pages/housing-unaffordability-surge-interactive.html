Why Tokyo worked

A city of 37 million people that kept housing (relatively) affordable.

1
Zoning reform, 1992

Japan adopted national zoning standards that override local opposition to density. Any neighbourhood can receive mixed-use, mid-rise development — no city council veto.

2
Build more, actually

Japan constructs ~800,000 homes per year. The UK — with a comparable population — builds ~200,000. Tokyo alone permitted more housing in 2022 than the entire state of California.

3
No NIMBYism, structurally

Japan's Building Standards Law sets construction rights nationally. Local governments cannot block by-right development. Neighbours can object but rarely prevail. The default is: build.

Data Sources

  • Demographia International Housing Affordability — Annual report measuring median multiple (median house price ÷ median household income) across major urban markets in Australia, Canada, China, Ireland, New Zealand, Singapore, UK, and USA; 2024 edition covers ~100 cities.
  • OECD Housing Prices Database — Tracks real and nominal house price indices and price-to-income ratios for OECD member countries from the 1970s through 2023, enabling long-run affordability trend analysis.
  • IMF Global Housing Watch — Quarterly tracker of house prices and price-to-income ratios for ~60 countries, updated through 2024, with historical series used to assess valuation gaps.
  • Numbeo Property Investment Index — Crowd-sourced database providing city-level price-to-income ratios and mortgage-as-percentage-of-income metrics for several hundred cities worldwide, updated continuously.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Price-to-income ratios are highly sensitive to which income measure is used — median vs. mean household income, and whether the denominator is one earner or two, can shift a city's ratio by 30–50%, making cross-source comparisons unreliable without standardization.
  • The ratio captures sticker price but not financing cost: a city can show a worsening ratio even as monthly mortgage payments stay flat (if rates fall), or appear stable while actual debt-service burdens spike (if rates rise) — both situations occurred between 2020 and 2023.
  • Urban boundary definitions vary widely across countries; a 'city' ratio for Paris (commune) vs. Île-de-France vs. Greater Paris reflects entirely different housing markets, so city-to-city comparisons require careful attention to the geographic unit used.
