Why does this happen?

Insulin was invented in 1921. The patent was sold to the University of Toronto for $1 — Frederick Banting, the lead researcher, believed no one should profit from a medicine people need to survive.

A century later, pharmaceutical companies have developed newer formulations ("analogue insulin") and secured new patents on those. In most countries, governments negotiate prices directly with manufacturers. In the United States, there is no federal price negotiation for most drugs.

"The molecule hasn't changed that much. The manufacturing process hasn't changed that much. What changed is the market structure."

The result: Americans with diabetes — about 8.4 million of whom use insulin — pay prices that no comparable country comes close to. About 9 million Americans ration their insulin doses, cutting them, skipping them, or buying from Canada or Mexico. People have died from this.

What's surprising

The most striking comparison isn't US vs Pakistan or US vs India — you'd expect richer countries to pay more. The jaw-dropper is US vs Germany, US vs France, US vs Japan. These are wealthy nations with sophisticated healthcare systems. They pay $7–$15. The US pays $98.70.

Canada, which shares a border and a similar GDP, pays $12. That's why people drive across the border to buy insulin — or order it online from Canadian pharmacies.

The data

All figures from the RAND Corporation / Health Affairs 2021 insulin pricing study, one of the most comprehensive cross-national comparisons available. Prices are retail, unadjusted for purchasing power — which, if anything, makes the US disparity look even larger on a PPP-adjusted basis.

Health Affairs, Vol. 40, No. 1 (2021)
Peer-reviewed companion analysis on insulin pricing disparities.

  • The RAND data reflects 2018 retail prices. Since then, US insulin list prices have changed materially: in 2023, all three major manufacturers (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, Sanofi) capped out-of-pocket costs at $35/month for insured patients, and biosimilar insulins entered the market at lower prices. The structural gap with other wealthy nations persists, but the most extreme figures are somewhat improved.
  • Prices are unadjusted for purchasing power parity. A PPP-adjusted comparison would make the US gap smaller relative to wealthier countries and larger relative to lower-income ones.
  • The '9 million Americans ration insulin' figure is from Herkert et al. (2019, Yale) and predates the 2023 cap legislation. Current rationing figures are likely lower but remain significant.
  • Non-RAND country prices (India, Pakistan, and others beyond the study's 56 countries) are estimated from WHO and IMS Health data and carry higher uncertainty.
