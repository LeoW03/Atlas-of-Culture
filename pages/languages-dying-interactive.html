02 — The Last Speakers

One Person.
One Language.

When the last speaker dies, something irreplaceable disappears — not just vocabulary, but an entire architecture of thought. These are some of the names we know.

Ubykh
Once spoken along the eastern Black Sea coast. Considered one of the phonologically richest languages ever documented — 84 distinct consonants, no language has more.
Last speaker: Tevfik Esenç. Died October 7, 1992, in Turkey.
Extinct 1992
0 speakers
Eyak
A Na-Dene language of the Pacific coast of Alaska. Eyak's grammar encoded ecological distinctions for which English has no equivalent — the precise relationship between a human and the land beneath them.
Last speaker: Marie Smith Jones. Died January 21, 2008, aged 89.
Extinct 2008
0 speakers
Yaghan
Spoken at the southern tip of South America — the southernmost language on Earth. Yaghan has words for experiences that no other language has named. Mamihlapinatapai: a shared look between two people, each wishing the other would initiate something they both desire.
Sole remaining speaker: Cristina Calderón, born 1928, Chile.

03 — The Acceleration

The Rate Is Not Slowing

Language extinction has always existed. What's new is the pace — and the mechanism. Not war or plague, but economic integration. When joining the global economy means abandoning your mother tongue, most people make a rational choice.

Each language lost is not simply a communication system going quiet. It is the permanent erasure of a cognitive framework — a way of segmenting reality, encoding memory, navigating time.

◈
Grammatical worlds
Many languages encode information English ignores entirely: whether the speaker witnessed an event directly, whether an action is complete, which direction a river flows relative to the speaker. These aren't words. They're mandatory categories of reality.
◈
Ecological knowledge
Indigenous languages often contain thousands of terms for local plants, animals, weather patterns, and farming techniques accumulated over millennia. When the language goes, so does the taxonomy — and the knowledge embedded in it.
◈
Shapes of time
Some languages have no tense — time is not linear but spatial. Others count time backward from a predicted event. Some have words for the specific grief of watching something you love become unfamiliar. These are not translatable. They are lost.
◈
Oral literature
Every language carries its own poetics: its songs, its stories, its ways of structuring narrative, the jokes that work only in that tongue. Transcription preserves words. It cannot preserve voice, timing, or the culture that made the joke land.

04 — The Pressure

Five Languages.
Half the World.

Language death is rarely random. It follows power. Five languages — each backed by a major state and economy — are responsible for the majority of documented extinctions. Speakers don't abandon their languages. They're pressed out of them.

The mechanism is consistent: dominant-language schooling, economic exclusion of minority speakers, migration to cities where the home language has no function. Within two generations, transmission breaks.

"A language is not just a system of communication.
It is a living map of a people's encounters with the world —
their fears, their loves, what they noticed and named."

— Wade Davis, anthropologist

Of the world's ~7,100 languages, fewer than 600 have more than 100,000 speakers. The rest are fragile. Most have no documentation. Many will vanish before anyone records them.

Data Sources

  • UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger — UNESCO's interactive database classifying ~2,500 languages across five endangerment levels (vulnerable to extinct), with the most recent print edition published in 2010 and the online atlas updated periodically.
  • Ethnologue (SIL International) — Comprehensive catalog of ~7,000 living languages with EGIDS (Expanded Graded Intergenerational Disruption Scale) vitality ratings from 0 (international) to 10 (extinct), updated annually.
  • Endangered Languages Project — A Google-supported collaborative platform cataloging endangered languages with geographic data, audio samples, and endangerment severity ratings drawn from linguist-contributed field research.
  • Glottolog — Max Planck Institute's authoritative genealogical classification of the world's languages and dialects, including endangerment status sourced from primary linguistic literature.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Endangerment classifications differ across sources — UNESCO uses a 9-factor vitality assessment, Ethnologue uses the EGIDS scale, and the Endangered Languages Project uses a separate 5-tier system — making direct cross-source comparisons unreliable.
  • Speaker counts are frequently based on decades-old national censuses or small-scale field surveys; for many languages in Papua New Guinea, the Amazon basin, and parts of sub-Saharan Africa, reliable data simply does not exist.
  • Language death is gradual and under-reported: a language may lose its last fluent speakers years before an 'extinct' designation appears in any database, meaning extinction rates derived from these sources likely understate actual loss.
