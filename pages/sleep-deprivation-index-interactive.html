Slide 03 — Culture

Sleep is Political

The data isn't just biology. It's culture. Three vignettes that explain why East Asia systematically under-sleeps — and one exception that proves the rule.

🇯🇵

Japan

「居眠り」inemuri — "sleeping while present"

In Japan, falling asleep at your desk doesn't get you fired — it signals dedication. You're so committed you work until exhaustion. Inemuri is socially sanctioned collapse. The paradox: the permission to nap publicly exists because the culture expects you to never truly rest. 7h 22min average. $138bn/yr in GDP lost.

🇰🇷

South Korea

빨리빨리 ppalli ppalli — "hurry hurry"

South Korea's ppalli ppalli culture demands speed in everything — delivery, work, life. Sleep is the enemy of productivity. To rest is to fall behind. The country rebuilt from war to tech powerhouse in a generation, but the cultural operating system still runs on urgency. 7h 51min. The body doesn't care about GDP.

🇪🇸

Spain / Mediterranean

siesta — an afternoon pause

The siesta was never laziness — it was circadian wisdom. A post-lunch rest aligned with the body's natural afternoon dip, then extended work into a cooler evening. But globalization killed it. Spain's siesta is largely a tourist myth now. What remains: a culture that still socializes late, sleeps late, and hasn't fully adopted Northern European schedules.

⚠️ The 7-Hour Mortality Cliff

Sleep scientist Matthew Walker's research (and broader epidemiology) shows a nonlinear relationship between sleep and mortality. Below 7 hours per night, the risks spike sharply: cardiovascular disease, cancer risk, immune suppression, cognitive decline. The human body was not optimized for inemuri. It was optimized for 7–9 uninterrupted hours.

Data Sources

  • RAND Corporation — Why Sleep Matters: Quantifying the Economic Costs of Insufficient Sleep — Landmark 2016 report estimating GDP losses from sleep deprivation across five countries (US, UK, Germany, Japan, Canada), including productivity and mortality modeling.
  • Walch et al. — A global quantification of 'normal' sleep schedules using smartphone data, Science Advances — Peer-reviewed 2016 study analyzing sleep duration, timing, and social jetlag across 100+ countries using anonymized data from the Entrain smartphone app.
  • OECD Time Use Database — Harmonized time-use survey data from OECD member countries capturing average daily minutes spent sleeping, covering survey waves from the 2000s–2020s.
  • American Academy of Sleep Medicine / Sleep Research Society — Consensus Statement on Recommended Sleep Duration — 2015 consensus defining the 7–9 hour adult sleep recommendation used as the benchmark threshold for classifying sleep deprivation in most comparative studies.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Most national averages rely on self-reported sleep duration, which consistently overestimates actual sleep by 30–60 minutes compared to actigraphy or polysomnography measurements — meaning true deprivation rates are likely higher than shown.
  • Smartphone and wearable-derived datasets (including the Walch et al. study) carry significant selection bias: users who track sleep skew younger, wealthier, and more health-conscious than the general population, potentially flattering averages in high-income countries.
  • Economic cost estimates vary widely by model: the RAND figures assume a direct link between short sleep and mortality/absenteeism, but causality is difficult to establish — poor health often causes short sleep rather than the reverse, which may inflate attributed costs.
