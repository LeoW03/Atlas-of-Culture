The measurement

The OECD tracks teacher salaries as a percentage of the average national wage — a measure that strips out cost-of-living differences and tells you directly: how does this society value its teachers relative to everyone else?

A ratio of 100% means teachers earn exactly the average wage. Luxembourg's 198% means teachers earn nearly twice the average. The US's 64% means teachers earn about two-thirds of what the typical American worker earns.

The correlation

Across OECD countries, teacher pay ratio correlates positively with PISA outcomes. Countries that pay teachers well tend to get better results. But the relationship is noisy — and the noise is the interesting part.

Luxembourg pays teachers the most in the world. It ranks in the middle of OECD countries on PISA. Money alone isn't the answer.

The countries that consistently outperform — Finland, Singapore, Japan, South Korea — share something beyond pay: teaching is a high-status, selective profession. Finland accepts fewer than 10% of teacher training applicants, from a pool that's already competitive. Singapore recruits from the top third of university graduates. The pay is part of the signal, but the selectivity is what creates the status.

The US case

The US sits below the trend line for its wealth level — meaning American students perform worse than you'd predict given US GDP per capita. Teacher pay is one factor. Teacher training program selectivity is another: most US programs accept the majority of applicants. The combination sends a clear signal about professional status.

This isn't a funding problem per se. The US spends more per student than most OECD countries. The issue is where the money goes and what it signals about the profession.

The outliers

South Korea pays teachers around 130% of average — above average but not extreme. Yet its PISA scores rank among the world's highest. The explanation is cultural: teaching has historically carried enormous social prestige in Korea, independent of salary.

Poland pays teachers around 92% of average — below parity — yet scores near the OECD average, consistently above the US. Strong teacher unions and a national curriculum may be doing work that pay alone doesn't.

These outliers suggest the real variable isn't pay but professional status — and pay is just one of the levers that creates or destroys it.

Caveats & methodology notes


  • The pay ratio measures salary relative to the national average wage — it captures status and competitiveness within a country, but does not reflect absolute purchasing power. A teacher in Luxembourg earning 198% of the average wage may have lower real purchasing power than one in a cheaper country earning 110%.
  • Non-OECD salary figures (Singapore, China, India, etc.) are sourced from national statistical publications rather than direct OECD measurement. Methodology varies across countries; treat these as indicative, not precise.
  • China's PISA data covers only four provinces (Shanghai, Beijing, Jiangsu, Zhejiang) — among the highest-performing regions in the country. It is not nationally representative.
