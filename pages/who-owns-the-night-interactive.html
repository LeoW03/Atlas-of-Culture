02 — The Peninsula Paradox

195 to 1.
Same peninsula.
Different worlds.

The Korean Peninsula is the starkest natural experiment in human history. Same climate. Same geography. Same culture. Split 70 years ago.

South Korea blazes at ~195 nW/cm²/sr — among the brightest countries on Earth. North Korea registers ~1. The border is visible from space as a hard line of darkness.

This isn't a map of light. It's a map of what happens when human freedom and economic systems diverge, written in photons.

04 — What the Light Tells Us

Reading the Dark

Light as economic signal · Energy access · Power and absence

Light correlates with almost everything we care about

Nighttime radiance tracks GDP per capita with remarkable fidelity. When economists need a proxy for economic activity in places with poor data, they use satellite lights. The relationship isn't perfect — it misses subsistence agriculture, informal economies — but it's close enough to matter.

The Gulf paradox: wealth without breadth

Kuwait, Qatar, and the UAE top the global rankings — not because they have large populations, but because energy is cheap and cities are dense. High radiance, low population density, oil wealth. These aren't the most complex economies; they're the most energy-profligate ones.

The darkness of conflict and control

The places with lowest light tell the most urgent stories:

  • North Korea~1 nW — political darkness
  • Central African Republic~2 nW — conflict/fragility
  • South Sudan~2 nW — youngest nation, ongoing crisis
  • Chad~3 nW — poverty + landlocked isolation
  • Afghanistan~4 nW — war, then Taliban darkness

North Korea is the anomaly. Chad is poor. North Korea is dark by design. The regime controls electricity as a mechanism of control — cities get power, rural areas almost nothing, the capital city (Pyongyang) gets occasional flickers to signal regime favor.

India: the brightness gap

India (~45 nW) represents the world's largest story of uneven electrification. Urban centers blaze; rural areas remain dim. As electrification expands, India's satellite signature is literally brightening year by year — one of the few countries where you can watch development happen in real time from orbit.

The moral weight of light

Access to electricity — and thus light — is correlated with virtually every dimension of human flourishing: literacy, child mortality, women's economic participation, life expectancy. The dark places on this map are not just dark. They're places where children do homework by candlelight, where hospitals run on generators, where the night itself is dangerous.

Light pollution may be a problem for astronomers in wealthy countries. For most of the world, the problem is the opposite.

Data Sources

  • NASA Black Marble (VNP46 VIIRS Product) — NASA's annual nighttime lights composites derived from the VIIRS Day/Night Band aboard the Suomi NPP and NOAA-20 satellites, corrected for stray light, lunar illumination, and cloud cover; primary satellite dataset used in most recent country/city comparisons (2012–present).
  • Falchi et al. (2016) — 'The New World Atlas of Artificial Night Sky Brightness', Science Advances — Peer-reviewed atlas modeling ground-level skyglow from VIIRS radiance data; estimates that 83% of the world's population lives under light-polluted skies and underpins most national/regional rankings cited in the scientific literature.
  • Earth Observation Group — VIIRS Nighttime Lights (Colorado School of Mines) — Processes raw VIIRS DNB satellite passes into monthly and annual composites with outlier filtering (fires, aurora); the standard research-grade nighttime radiance dataset used to compare countries and cities globally.
  • International Dark-Sky Association — Light Pollution Research Hub — Synthesizes satellite and ground-based measurements to characterize ecological and human-health impacts of artificial light at night (ALAN), with country- and region-level summaries.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Satellite sensors measure upward radiance at ~1:30 AM local time — a single nightly snapshot, not an average across all hours. Early-evening lighting (which matters most for human exposure) is not captured.
  • The VIIRS Day/Night Band is most sensitive to blue-green wavelengths, so the LED conversion underway in most cities since ~2015 causes measured radiance to rise even when total lumens stay constant, complicating before/after comparisons.
  • Country-level rankings conflate population density with lighting intensity: small, densely built nations (e.g. Singapore, Kuwait) rank near the top partly because there is little dark rural land to dilute the average, not necessarily because individual light fixtures are brighter.
