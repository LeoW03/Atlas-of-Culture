How Gulf states bought their way into the global mobility club

The mechanism

Passport power isn't just about wealth — it's about diplomatic relationships. The Gulf states discovered this and deployed capital accordingly. Starting in the early 2010s, the UAE began offering visa-free access to citizens from dozens of countries, with the implicit expectation of reciprocity. When a country sends tourists and workers to Dubai, it has incentive to remove the friction.

By 2015, the UAE had unlocked most of the Americas and large parts of Asia. By 2020, post-Abraham Accords, the EU followed — granting Emiratis visa-free access across Schengen.

"The UAE passport improved by 146 countries in 19 years. The US improved by 26."

Saudi Arabia's sudden jump

Saudi Arabia's trajectory is even more abrupt. In 2020, the Saudi passport opened 76 countries. By 2023: 179. The near-doubling coincided with MBS's diplomatic opening — normalizing relations with Israel's neighbors, restoring ties with Iran, rejoining ASEAN diplomatic circles, and aggressively pursuing bilateral visa agreements as part of Vision 2030's tourism strategy.

What Western passport power actually buys

The remaining gap — Germany at 191, Japan at 193 — is concentrated in a handful of countries with complex relationships: North Korea, Russia (post-2022), Iran, and a few others. For practical global travel, the difference between a UAE passport (185) and a German passport (191) is six countries, most of which few people visit.

The question the data raises: if Gulf states can engineer passport parity with the West in a generation, what did Western passport power actually represent — diplomatic trust, or just historical inertia?

Data & methodology

Sources

Henley Passport Index
Primary source for all current and historical passport power rankings. Counts countries where a passport holder can travel without obtaining a visa in advance (visa-free or visa-on-arrival). Updated quarterly; figures used here reflect 2025 Q1 rankings.
Arton Capital Passport Index
Cross-referenced for historical timeline data (2000–2015), which Henley's public archive does not fully cover.

Caveats & methodology notes

  • Visa-free access counts include both visa-free entry and visa-on-arrival — two meaningfully different experiences. A country where you collect a stamp at the airport (VOA) is counted the same as one with no entry barrier at all.
  • Historical data before 2015 is partially reconstructed from archived Henley reports and third-party tracking. UAE figures for 2000–2010 carry ±3 country uncertainty.
  • Saudi Arabia's 2023 jump reflects both bilateral agreements and counting methodology changes; some of the gain may reflect reclassification of e-visa programs as "visa-on-arrival" equivalents.
  • Rankings vary slightly between index providers (Henley, Arton, VisaGuide) due to different counting rules. This data uses Henley's methodology consistently.
