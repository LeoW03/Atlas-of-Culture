Does time in school predict outcomes?

Finland: 593 hours.
US: 1,016 hours.
Finland scores higher.

Finnish students spend the fewest hours in school of any OECD country — yet consistently score near the top on PISA. American students spend nearly twice as long in class and score below average for their wealth level. Something doesn't add up.

What the data actually shows

Four things worth understanding before drawing conclusions.

01

The measurement

PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment) tests 15-year-olds in reading, math, and science across 80+ countries every three years. Instructional hours come from the OECD's Education at a Glance — the number of hours students are legally required to be in class annually.

Neither measure is perfect. PISA captures a narrow slice. Instructional hours say nothing about what happens inside that time.

02

Why hours don't predict outcomes

The regression R² across this dataset is roughly 0.06 — hours explain about 6% of the variance in PISA scores. That's noise. China scores 561 with 960 hours. Poland scores 516 with 635. Singapore scores 569 with 831.

What correlates more strongly: teacher selection and pay, curriculum coherence, early childhood investment, and socioeconomic equity within the school system.

03

What Finland actually did

In the 1970s Finland reformed its teacher training to require a master's degree — and made teaching the most competitive profession in the country. Teachers are trusted professionals, not script-followers. Homework is minimal. School starts at age 7.

Less time, deeper learning. The hours aren't the point.

04

The US case

At 1,016 hours per year, the US sits near the top of the OECD in instructional time — and squarely in the middle on PISA, scoring 497. For a country at its wealth level and investment in education infrastructure, this is underperformance.

The cause isn't insufficient time. It's inequity: a funding system tied to local property taxes that produces dramatic gaps between rich and poor districts. More hours in a broken system aren't the fix.
