02 — The Correlation

Age vs. Fertility

Every additional year of median age correlates with a lower fertility rate. The curve is steep. Japan and Niger occupy opposite ends of the same axis. South Korea's 0.78 TFR is the fastest demographic collapse ever recorded.

Demographics are destiny. It's a cliché because it's true. The median age of a population predicts everything: its labor force size, its pension burden, its appetite for risk, its political temperament. Countries with young populations consume. Countries with old populations preserve.

"The fastest demographic collapse in recorded history isn't happening in a country at war. It's happening in South Korea — at peace, prosperous, and emptying out."

South Korea's total fertility rate of 0.78 means the country is replacing roughly 37% of its population each generation. No war, no plague — just the arithmetic of modernization pushed to its extreme. Women choosing careers, cities, independence. Each individual decision rational. The aggregate: civilizational contraction.

On the other end: Niger. Median age 15. Total fertility rate 6.8. Every family with six children means a population that doubles in a generation. This is not just a statistic — it's an economic and political pressure system. A country where more than half the population is under 18 needs schools, jobs, infrastructure at a rate no government can sustain.

Japan sits between these extremes — not as extreme as Korea, but facing its own reckoning. In 2023, Japan's working-age population shrank by 800,000 people. The country is importing workers it once refused to admit, extending retirement ages, automating everything it can. It's a preview of what Germany, Italy, and Spain will face in the coming decade.

The economic logic is unavoidable. Young countries have more workers per retiree. Old countries have fewer. At some ratio, the math breaks the pension system. At some age, innovation slows. At some fertility rate, recovery becomes impossible within any political timeframe.

03 — What It Means

Three Different Futures

Each extreme of the demographic spectrum faces a distinct economic challenge. None of them is simple. None of them is solved.

🇯🇵
Japan
49
The Shrinking Workforce
Japan lost 800,000 working-age people in 2023 alone. By 2050, one in three Japanese citizens will be over 65. The country that built its postwar economy on cheap, disciplined labor now can't find workers for its convenience stores. Immigration — long taboo — is now policy. Automation is survival.
🇰🇷
South Korea
0.78
The Fastest Collapse Ever
South Korea's TFR of 0.78 is the lowest ever recorded in a large economy — lower than during wars, famines, or plagues. It's the product of extreme housing costs, brutal work culture, and the highest private education spending on earth. Seoul's population could halve by 2100. No country has reversed a decline this steep.
🇳🇪
Niger
6.8
The Youth Bulge
Niger's TFR of 6.8 produces a population pyramid unlike any other. More than half the country is under 18. The government would need to build a new school every day to keep pace. The demographic dividend — the economic windfall young populations can unlock — requires jobs. Right now, the jobs aren't there. The pressure has nowhere to go.

Data Sources

  • UN World Population Prospects 2024 — The UN Population Division's authoritative dataset on global fertility rates, age-structure projections, and child population counts by country and region, with estimates through 2100.
  • UNICEF State of the World's Children 2023 — UNICEF's annual flagship report providing country-level data on the under-18 population, regional demographic concentrations, and trends in child population share.
  • World Bank Open Data — Fertility Rate (Total Births per Woman) — World Bank series of total fertility rate estimates by country from 1960–2022, sourced from national statistical offices and the UN Population Division.
  • Our World in Data — Fertility Rate — Aggregated long-run fertility and child population data with interactive breakdowns by income group, region, and historical period, drawing on UN WPP and historical demographic sources.

Caveats & Methodology

  • Fertility rate estimates for sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia rely heavily on household surveys (DHS, MICS) rather than complete civil registration systems, introducing sampling error and potential underreporting of births — margins of uncertainty can exceed ±0.3 children per woman in data-sparse countries.
  • The definition of 'child' varies across sources: UN demographic projections use under-15 as the standard age cutoff for youth dependency ratios, while UNICEF uses under-18 — this produces meaningfully different figures for regional child population shares and should be checked when comparing datasets.
  • National averages mask sharp sub-national variation; in large, high-fertility countries like Nigeria, Niger, and the DRC, urban-rural fertility gaps of 2–3 children per woman are common, so country-level figures may misrepresent where children actually live within a country.
